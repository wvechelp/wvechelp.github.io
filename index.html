HydroCORE

Studying impacts of hydropower infrastructure on Andean headwaters

Hydropower is a well-known and popular alternative to the burning of fossil fuels due to its simplicity and relatively low cost. Unfortunately, these hydropower installations tend to have an impact on the environment. The HydroCORE project uses this notice as a basis to perform its activities, while using the Paute river basin in Cuenca (Ecuador) as study area. To communicate the findings, a project-specific website has been developed for HydroCORE.

T2021 Report Scheldt Estuary

Reporting on the state of the Scheldt estuary in the period 2016-2021

The Scheldt river finds it origin in France and continues its path through Belgium and the Netherlands, where it connects to the North Sea. Tidal activity runs as deep as the city of Ghent, where sluices prevent further intrusion of the tide and help maintain a stable water level within the city. The wide variety of activities around and within the Scheldt estuary basin affects the inherent characteristics of the river, though multiple mitigation strategies are in place to (continue to) improve the functioning of the Scheldt estuary. Every six years, an extensive T-report brings the monitoring data of the past six years together and evaluates the condition of the Scheldt estuary against a set of predefined criteria. The latest version covers the period 2016-2021 (T2021) and is being written by HKV, Antea Group, Waardenburg Ecology, and Ghent University. More information can be found on the website of VNSC (in Dutch) and on the Scheldemonitor (the platform for all Scheldt-related information).

Topic models to classify brain disorders

Applying topic models to look for patterns in fMRI data

The human brain is a complex structure and deviations can occur both at the structural and functional level. Deviations at the functional level (e.g., depression, schizophrenia, bipolar disorder) tend to be diagnosed based on interviews and associated symptom scores. More objective techniques to diagnose patients are desired, one of which is the use of functional neuroimaging techniques (e.g., EEG, PET, fMRI). The temporal profile of these fMRI signals are unique, though expected to be consistent among subjects of the same patient group. These patterns can be assessed through an alternative representation of the data as a bipartite network (signal-subject) and cluster inference via topic models. The obtained results show that clustering of patients based on the fMRI signals is possible, but suggest that there is another factor that steers the clustering (i.e. the infered clusters did not represent the known patient groups). More information of the project (available data, methodology, results) can be found in the report (UGent only) while the underlying scripts can be found on GitHub.

Special Issue

At the moment, I am acting as a Guest Editor for the Special Issue Biodiversity Assessment and Conservation in High-Altitude Freshwater Systems within the journal Water. This special issue aims to seek contributions reporting on the biodiversity of high-altitude freshwater systems in both developed and developing regions, including recommendations on how to manage and/or conserve the considered system sustainably. The deadline for manuscript submissions is 31 December 2025.

About me

As an environmental data scientist with a background in freshwater ecology, I am driven by the desire to understand natural processes and to identify synergies between nature and humanity. I have experience in field-based monitoring and data-driven modelling, along with a healthy interest in data analysis and visualisation. While it might be considered controversial, I strongly believe that we can learn a lot about freshwater ecology from purely observational data without completely understanding the underlying processes.
